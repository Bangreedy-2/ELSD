\chapter*{Conclusions}

The detailed analysis conducted in the first phase of this project has confirmed that while the digital manufacturing industry has advanced significantly in terms of hardware, the software infrastructure for machine control remains rooted in the mid-20th century. The reliance on G-code as the primary interface for Computer Numerical Control (CNC) and additive manufacturing systems creates a significant barrier to efficiency, customization, and safety.

The Problem-Based Learning (PBL) investigation highlighted three critical issues: the lack of readability and modifiability of machine instructions, the absence of abstraction which hides design intent, and the steep learning curve required to debug or optimize manufacturing processes manually. These issues affect a wide spectrum of users, from hobbyists in fabrication laboratories to engineers in industrial settings.

The proposed solution—a Domain-Specific Language (DSL)—emerges as a necessary and viable innovation. By introducing high-level programming concepts such as variables, loops, functions, and semantic operations into the manufacturing workflow, the DSL promises to transform machine instructions from cryptic data streams into readable, maintainable software code. This approach not only simplifies the creation of complex toolpaths but also introduces a compilation step where safety checks and optimizations can be performed automatically, reducing the risk of costly machine failures.

The comparative analysis demonstrates that while raw G-code offers control and CAD/CAM offers visualization, neither provides the programmable flexibility required for modern agile manufacturing or experimental research. The proposed DSL bridges this gap, offering the best of both worlds: high-level abstraction with deterministic low-level output.

In conclusion, the domain analysis and problem definition phase has laid a solid foundation for the development of the DSL. The functional requirements and constraints identified herein provide a clear roadmap for the next phase of the project: the formal specification of the language syntax and the implementation of the prototype compiler. This tool has the potential to significantly streamline the prototyping workflow and empower users to leverage the full capabilities of their manufacturing hardware.
